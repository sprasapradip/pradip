PLC-Based Industrial Automation System

Engineering Design & Implementation Report

Project Overview

This project involved designing and commissioning a Programmable Logic Controller (PLC) system to fully automate the operation of industrial equipment at a manufacturing facility. The system replaced manual switching and analogue relay logic with a structured, programmable control architecture that handles equipment startup sequencing, continuous process monitoring, and emergency safety response.

Prior to implementation, operators manually cycled equipment on and off based on visual gauges and shift-change checklists. That worked well enough for years, but as production volumes grew, so did the number of missed readings and near-miss safety events. The PLC system addressed all of this directly.

Objectives

The system was built to meet three core operational needs:

  • Automate equipment startup, shutdown, and load-sequencing to reduce operator error and response time.

  • Monitor process variables (temperature, pressure, flow rate, and motor current) in real time, with automatic fault detection and alarm generation.

  • Enforce safety interlocks that shut down equipment immediately when sensor readings exceed predefined safe operating limits.

  • Log operational data continuously for post-shift analysis and regulatory compliance reporting.

System Architecture

The control system is built around a Siemens S7-1200 series PLC, chosen for its compact footprint, integrated Ethernet port, and compatibility with the facility's existing PROFINET network infrastructure. The CPU handles all ladder logic and function block execution, while a distributed I/O rack at the machine floor picks up sensor signals and drives field devices.

Control logic was developed in TIA Portal using a combination of Ladder Diagram (LD) for safety interlock routines and Structured Text (ST) for the PID loop calculations and data logging functions. Separating these kept the safety logic easy to audit while keeping the more complex math manageable.

A touchscreen HMI panel (Siemens TP700 Comfort) sits at the operator station and provides a live process overview, manual override controls, and a fault history screen. Operators can acknowledge alarms, adjust setpoints within permitted ranges, and view the last 30 days of trend data without leaving the workstation.

Key Features & Functions

Automated Startup & Shutdown Sequencing

Equipment powers up in a fixed sequence with built-in time delays between stages to prevent current surges and mechanical shock. The shutdown sequence mirrors this in reverse, ensuring actuators fully travel to safe positions before upstream equipment de-energises.

Real-Time Process Monitoring

Twelve analogue input channels feed process variable readings into the PLC every 100 ms. The system compares each reading against high and low setpoint limits and writes a fault flag if any value drifts outside range. Operators see this on the HMI instantly. A persistent log records every reading to an SD card in CSV format for shift-end review.

Safety Interlock System

Emergency stops, overtemperature switches, pressure relief signals, and motor overload contacts are all wired into hardwired safety circuits as well as read by the PLC. The ladder logic enforces a safety matrix: if any two critical conditions occur simultaneously, the full system trips to a safe state and cannot restart until faults are manually cleared and acknowledged.

Implementation & Commissioning

The project ran over eight weeks from initial design to handover. The first two weeks were spent on I/O mapping, panel layout design, and writing the initial control logic offline. Weeks three and four covered panel build and factory acceptance testing using a simulated process signal generator.

Site installation and live commissioning took place over two weeks, working alongside the facility's electrical team. The final two weeks involved operator training, setpoint tuning, and supervised production runs before formal sign-off.

One challenge during commissioning was unexpected electrical noise on two analogue input channels from a nearby variable frequency drive. This was resolved by replacing single-ended wiring with shielded twisted-pair cable and adding a software averaging filter in the scan cycle. The fix eliminated false alarm trips that had appeared during initial testing.

Results & Outcomes

After three months of live operation, the system performed consistently within design parameters. Equipment startup time dropped from an average of 14 minutes under manual operation to under 4 minutes with automatic sequencing. The facility recorded zero safety trips caused by missed operator readings in the post-commissioning period, compared with three recorded incidents in the previous quarter.

  • Equipment startup time reduced from ~14 minutes (manual) to under 4 minutes (automatic).

  • Zero sensor-related safety incidents in the first 90 days post-commissioning.

  • Shift-end reporting time cut by roughly 40% due to automatic data logging.

  • Operators reported higher confidence in equipment status and alarm reliability.

Technologies & Tools Used

  • PLC Hardware: Siemens S7-1200 (CPU 1214C) with ET 200SP distributed I/O

  • HMI: Siemens TP700 Comfort Panel (WinCC Flexible)

  • Programming Environment: TIA Portal V17

  • Programming Languages: Ladder Diagram (LD), Structured Text (ST), Function Block Diagram (FBD)

  • Communication Protocol: PROFINET / Ethernet

  • Field Devices: RTD temperature sensors, pressure transducers, flow meters, motor contactors, solenoid valves

  • Documentation: AutoCAD Electrical (panel schematics), Excel (I/O list and setpoint register)

Reflection

This project reinforced how much of industrial automation work happens before writing a single line of logic: the I/O mapping, the P&ID review, the conversations with operators about what actually causes problems on shift. The PLC code itself was relatively straightforward once those inputs were clear.

The noise issue during commissioning was also a good reminder that physical signal integrity often matters as much as the logic design. A system that works perfectly in simulation can behave differently the moment it's wired into a real plant environment.
